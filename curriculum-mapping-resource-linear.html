Section 1

Knowledge foundations

Effective curriculum mapping draws on several interconnected bodies of knowledge. Rigorous design holds all of them in view simultaneously — and produces work that surface-level template completion cannot. These knowledge areas are not supplementary to the technical competencies; they are what gives the competencies their meaning.

⚖️

Equity and social justice frameworks

Understanding how structural inequity — racism, ableism, colonialism, classism — shapes what is taught, how it is assessed, who is welcomed, and how success is defined. This is the ethical foundation of curriculum mapping, not a supplementary concern. Inclusive curriculum design requires examining the structure of the curriculum itself: whose knowledge is treated as standard, whose experiences are marginalised, and whose communication styles are rewarded. Every outcome, assessment, strategy, and participation structure is a site for asking: who benefits, who is burdened, and who is being erased.

🧠

The science of learning and instruction

The cognitive and motivational mechanisms underlying effective learning — including cognitive load theory, retrieval practice, spaced practice, interleaving, feedback, and the conditions supporting transfer. This is what separates evidence-based design from intuition-based design. Without it, curricula routinely emphasise engagement over retention, content coverage over mastery, and activity over transfer. Expertise develops when information becomes organised into efficient schemas in long-term memory; curriculum sequencing must support that transition from effortful processing to automatic performance.

🔄

Adult learning theory

The foundational principles of how adults learn — including self-direction, the role of prior experience, internal motivation, problem-centred orientation, and relevance. Adults are most engaged when learning connects directly to their work and lives, when they have genuine autonomy over their educational experience, and when prior knowledge is respected rather than bypassed. Designing without this grounding produces curricula that treat adults as blank slates and lose their engagement accordingly.

🗺️

Curriculum theory

The broader intellectual history of what curriculum is for — including the distinction between intended, enacted, and learned curriculum, the concept of the hidden curriculum, and the question of whose knowledge gets legitimised through curricular choices. A curriculum map may be technically sound, but what gets enacted can differ because of instructor judgment, institutional constraints, or learner realities. What learners actually take away may differ again. Without curriculum theory, mapping risks being technically competent but politically naive — producing documents that look coherent while leaving deeper assumptions untouched.

♿

Universal design for learning and accessibility

Not as a checklist of accommodations, but as a design framework grounded in the understanding that learner variability is the norm, not the exception. The three UDL principles — multiple means of engagement, representation, and action and expression — need to be understood at the level of why they exist. Paired with a social model of disability: barriers are located in environments that weren't designed with the full range of human variability in mind, not in individuals. Identifying barriers requires knowing what to look for — which is why these frameworks are foundational rather than optional.

📐

Assessment theory

The principles of validity, reliability, and fairness — particularly construct validity, which anchors UDL-informed assessment design. Understanding what an assessment is actually measuring, and what it may be inadvertently measuring instead, requires genuine grounding here. A common design failure is letting the format become the hidden target: learners scored on presentation style, memory load, or language mechanics rather than the intended construct. An assessment that "technically passes" alignment review can still fail learners when it conflates the construct with the channel for demonstrating it.

🏛️

Instructional systems design models

Familiarity with major ISD frameworks — ADDIE, backward design, Understanding by Design, and their critiques — as a base from which to work critically. The practitioner needs to know these models well enough to apply them and well enough to know where they fall short for equity and inclusion. Backward design — beginning with desired outcomes and working backward to assessment and then instruction — is particularly relevant to curriculum mapping because it prevents content coverage from driving the design rather than learning goals.

🌐

Sociocultural dimensions of learning

Learning is not a purely cognitive event. It is shaped by identity, belonging, power, and the social conditions of the learning environment. Stereotype threat, communities of practice, and the zone of proximal development are all relevant — and they affect which learners thrive and which accumulate avoidable barriers. A curriculum that is cognitively well-sequenced but socially unwelcoming will still produce inequitable outcomes, because motivation, persistence, and engagement are shaped by whether learners feel they belong in the learning community.

🔧

Organisational learning and change theory

Curriculum mapping almost always happens inside organisations. Practitioners need to understand how organisations learn, resist change, and develop capacity — including the conditions under which curriculum reform actually sticks versus produces a document that gets filed and forgotten. Change theory is not optional; it determines whether design work has lasting effect. Organisations that invest in curriculum mapping without the organisational change literacy to embed the work typically find their maps treated as compliance artefacts rather than living systems.

How these relate: The foundational knowledge is what gives the competencies their why. A practitioner who can write outcomes but lacks grounding in assessment theory will write outcomes that sound appropriate but do not hold up to scrutiny. A practitioner who can identify barriers but lacks grounding in equity frameworks will identify the obvious ones and miss the structural ones. The gap between technically organised and genuinely inclusive is where this knowledge makes the difference.
Section 2

Core competencies

These are the applied, demonstrable capacities a curriculum mapping practitioner brings to the work. They are not equally weighted: barrier identification, assessment design, and stakeholder engagement tend to be where the most consequential decisions happen — and where the gap between technically competent and genuinely inclusive practice is widest.

1 Equity-centred needs analysis

The ability to investigate not just what learners need to know or do, but who the learners are, what conditions they are learning under, what barriers exist before instruction begins, and whose knowledge and experience the curriculum currently centres. This means going beyond task analysis into structural analysis — examining how power, access, and identity shape the learning environment before a single outcome is written.

2 Learning outcome development

Writing outcomes that describe genuine capability rather than content exposure — and doing so in ways that do not embed unnecessary barriers in the language itself. This includes distinguishing the construct being assessed from the method used to assess it. Strong outcomes use observable verbs (analyse, apply, design, evaluate), describe authentic performance in realistic contexts, and are specific enough to anchor assessment without being so narrow they exclude legitimate demonstration modes.

3 Alignment mapping

Building and maintaining coherent vertical and horizontal alignment — outcomes to assessments to instructional strategies, across levels and across the programme — while simultaneously checking that alignment at each node is accessible, not just logically connected. Vertical alignment ensures earlier courses build genuine foundations for later ones. Horizontal alignment keeps parallel courses from sending contradictory signals about what competent performance looks like. A curriculum can be logically aligned and still exclude learners if it relies too much on inaccessible reading load, unclear directions, or a single form of expression.

4 Assessment design

Designing assessments that validly measure what they claim to measure, offer multiple means of demonstrating competence where the means is not the construct, and do not systematically disadvantage learners based on disability, language background, or cultural context. Reliability comes from clear criteria and shared rubrics — not from forcing every learner into one rigid format. The best designs standardise the criteria for evidence while allowing variation in how evidence is produced when the construct permits it.

5 Instructional strategy selection

Choosing strategies based on evidence about how people learn — not habit, convention, or ease of delivery — and sequencing them to build genuine capability progressively. The inclusive dimension is knowing which strategies broaden access and which inadvertently narrow it. Early stages benefit from clear modelling, low-stakes practice, and explicit scaffolding. Later stages can shift toward collaboration, problem-solving, and independent performance. The strategy should always match the kind of thinking or doing the outcome requires.

6 Barrier identification and removal

The ability to read a curriculum design — outcomes, assessments, participation structures, sequencing, materials — and locate where barriers to access and learning are embedded, then make principled decisions about which barriers are necessary to the construct and which are removable without compromising rigour. This requires grounding in both disability studies and equity frameworks: without both, practitioners identify obvious surface barriers and miss the structural ones that do the most harm.

7 Stakeholder engagement and co-design facilitation

Working with subject matter experts, organisational leaders, and learners themselves in ways that produce better design decisions — which requires managing productive tensions between expertise and lived experience, between organisational priorities and learner needs, and between what is feasible and what is right. Effective facilitation keeps groups moving from opinion to evidence, ensures people with less formal authority are not crowded out, and makes decision criteria explicit so the process is fair even when not every opinion carries equal weight.

8 Systemic coherence evaluation

The ability to step back from individual components and assess whether the curriculum functions as a coherent system — not just whether each part is defensible in isolation, but whether the whole produces what it is intended to produce for the range of people it is meant to serve. A curriculum is not coherent if it only works for some learners. Systemic coherence includes equity: if the design depends on hidden prior knowledge, one communication style, or a narrow cultural norm, the whole system is less functional even if individual components appear sound.

A practical hierarchy: Map outcomes first, then assessments, then instructional strategies, then materials. Outcomes define the target; assessments provide evidence; instructional strategies create practice and support; materials supply what is needed for success. If any link in this chain is weak, the whole chain becomes less trustworthy — a well-written outcome is not aligned if the assessment measures something else.
Section 3

Design methodology

Effective curriculum mapping is structured, iterative, and multidimensional. The stages below represent a comprehensive approach; depth and scope vary with context, available resources, and organisational readiness. Understanding the full methodology clarifies what is gained — or lost — when particular stages are abbreviated.

1 Stage 1 Equity-centred needs analysis Understanding who the learners are and what conditions they are learning under before designing anything
FoundationalCollaborative

Before any design begins, the curriculum must be understood in its full context. This means investigating who the learners are — their identities, prior experiences, conditions of learning, and the barriers they face before instruction starts. It also means examining whose knowledge the existing curriculum centres, and which learners have historically been underserved by it.

This stage draws on both qualitative and quantitative data: interviews, surveys, focus groups, performance data, and community insight. The intersectionality of identities within the learner population must be integrated from the outset — failing to consider how overlapping social categories affect individual experiences leads to an incomplete picture and, consequently, inadequate design.

What this stage produces
  • A structural analysis of the learner population, including known barriers and conditions of learning
  • Documentation of whose knowledge and experience the curriculum currently centres
  • A shared understanding among stakeholders of what equitable means in this specific context
  • Prioritised learner needs that will anchor outcome development and assessment design
Note: Needs analysis conducted without genuine community and learner participation tends to reflect the assumptions of the most powerful voices in the room — producing designs that look coherent on paper but do not hold up in practice.
2 Stage 2 Learning outcome development Defining what learners will genuinely be able to do — in terms that can be assessed and taught
DesignPrecision-focused

Outcomes state what learners should know, do, or demonstrate by the end of a course or programme. Strong outcomes are student-centred, measurable, and written with observable verbs — analyse, apply, design, evaluate — rather than vague terms like understand or appreciate. In curriculum mapping, outcomes become the anchor for deciding where each competency is introduced, practised, and assessed.

A useful development process begins with programme goals, stakeholder needs, and the actual work learners are preparing to do. Critically, the language of outcomes should not itself introduce unnecessary barriers — complex nominalisations, culturally specific assumptions, or language that presumes particular academic backgrounds can exclude learners before instruction begins.

What this stage produces
  • Programme-level outcomes mapped to authentic performance expectations
  • Course-level outcomes aligned to specific learning experiences
  • A clear distinction between the construct being assessed and the method used to assess it
  • A developmental sequence showing how outcomes progress from introduction to reinforcement to mastery
Note: A healthy curriculum map shows outcomes introduced early, reinforced through meaningful practice, and assessed for mastery near the end of the programme. Learners should not be expected to demonstrate competence before they have had sufficient opportunity to develop it.
3 Stage 3 Alignment mapping Making the relationships among outcomes, assessments, and instruction visible and inspectable
SystematicStructural

Alignment mapping ensures that outcomes, assessments, instructional strategies, and materials fit together as a coherent chain. It runs backward from learning goals rather than forward from content coverage. The point is not just to connect pieces logically, but to ensure that each piece produces evidence of the intended learning.

Vertical alignment means outcomes build across levels in a sequence that moves from simpler to more complex performance. Horizontal alignment means that parallel courses coordinate expectations and practices so learners encounter consistent, reinforcing signals about what competent performance looks like. Accessibility must be checked at every node — a course can be logically aligned and still exclude learners if it relies on inaccessible formats, unclear directions, or rigid timing.

What this stage produces
  • A programme-level alignment matrix showing outcomes distributed across courses with introduce, reinforce, and master designations
  • Node-by-node verification: outcome, assessment, strategy, access, and sequence checks
  • Identification of gaps, unintended redundancy, and assessments that demand skills before they have been developed
  • A living document designed for regular review as evidence accumulates
Note: Alignment mapping is not a one-time planning task. It requires regular review using learner performance data, faculty feedback, and changes in workplace expectations. The best programmes treat the alignment map as a living system, not a completed deliverable.
4 Stage 4 Assessment design Ensuring assessment measures the intended competence — equitably and with multiple valid pathways
High-stakesEquity-critical

Assessment design is where curriculum quality is won or lost. It determines whether a programme truly measures the intended competence, whether learners can demonstrate that competence through more than one valid pathway, and whether the assessment process is equitable for disabled learners, multilingual learners, and learners from different cultural backgrounds.

Validity is the degree to which an assessment actually measures the construct it claims to measure. A common failure is letting the format become the hidden target — learners scored on presentation polish, memory load, or decoding demands rather than the intended skill. Formative support — scaffolding before the high-stakes moment — is one of the most effective ways to ensure the assessment measures achievement rather than first exposure or guesswork.

What this stage produces
  • Assessments with clear construct validity — measuring the intended outcome, not a proxy for it
  • Multiple demonstration modes where the mode is not the construct
  • Plain and precise instructions, consistent formatting, and transparent criteria available in advance
  • Formative practice opportunities aligned to the summative task
  • Rubrics that distinguish essential evidence from format preferences unrelated to the construct
Note: Reliability and flexibility are not opposites when assessment is well designed. Reliability comes from clear criteria and shared rubrics — not from forcing every learner into one rigid format. Standardise the criteria for evidence; allow variation in how evidence is produced when the construct permits it.
5 Stage 5 Instructional strategy selection Choosing and sequencing strategies based on evidence about how people learn
Evidence-basedSequencing

Instructional strategies are chosen because they support the kind of learning the outcome requires — not because they are familiar, easy to manage, or simply available. If the outcome requires performance, active strategies that give learners a chance to rehearse the target behaviour are more effective than passive exposure. If the outcome requires conceptual understanding, explanation, worked examples, and guided discussion may be appropriate, paired with retrieval opportunities.

Progressive sequencing builds capability over time: early stages work best with clear modelling, low-stakes practice, and explicit scaffolding; later stages shift toward collaboration, problem-solving, and independent performance. The inclusive dimension is knowing which strategies broaden access — multiple engagement modes, predictable structures, clear directions — and which inadvertently narrow it, such as strategies that depend exclusively on rapid verbal exchange.

What this stage produces
  • A strategy sequence aligned to the learning demand of each outcome
  • Visibility into which strategies broaden access and which silently narrow it
  • Progressive scaffolding from modelled to guided to independent practice
  • Multiple entry points and participation modes built into the design, not added afterward
Note: The best curriculum maps show not just what is taught, but how learning is scaffolded, how practice accumulates, and how inclusion is protected across the sequence. A common failure is choosing a strategy because it is easy to manage rather than because it supports the intended capability.
6 Stage 6 Stakeholder engagement and co-design Bringing multiple perspectives together to produce better, more equitable design decisions
CollaborativePower-aware

Curriculum designs are stronger when informed by multiple perspectives. Subject matter experts know what competence looks like. Organisational leaders know what must be sustained operationally. Learners know where friction, confusion, or exclusion will actually occur. When those perspectives are brought together well, the resulting map is more accurate, more usable, and more equitable. Without co-design, curriculum work often defaults to the assumptions of the most powerful voice in the room.

Effective facilitation creates conditions where everyone can contribute meaningfully — setting shared norms, clarifying decision points, separating ideas from roles, and ensuring people with less formal authority are not crowded out. Power differences are always present in co-design processes, even when everyone is invited equally. Making decision criteria explicit, using accessible language, and ensuring dissent can be voiced without penalty are non-negotiable practices.

What this stage produces
  • Design decisions that are technically sound, organisationally workable, and accessible to learners
  • Documentation of what was decided, why, and what tradeoff it reflects
  • A curriculum map treated as a living artefact that can be revised after implementation feedback
  • Sustained trust among stakeholders that their contributions meaningfully shaped the design
Note: Token consultation — where stakeholders are asked for feedback after main decisions are already made — reduces trust and makes implementation harder. Expert dominance, leader capture, and unstructured discussion are equally common failure modes. The real aim is not agreement: it is a curriculum that is both feasible and worthy of the learners it serves.
7 Stage 7 Systemic coherence evaluation and continuous improvement Assessing whether the curriculum functions as a whole system — and for the full range of learners it serves
Systems-levelIterative

Systemic coherence evaluation asks whether the curriculum functions as a unified architecture — not just whether each component is defensible in isolation. This requires stepping back and asking how the curriculum behaves as a network of decisions: whether outcomes build logically, whether assessments measure what instruction actually prepares learners for, and whether the design creates a stable, navigable path from entry to completion.

A curriculum is not coherent if it only works for some learners. Signs of weak systemic coherence include outcomes too numerous or vague to be meaningful, assessments measuring the same narrow skill while other important abilities are never addressed, courses that feel isolated from one another, and support services that exist in theory but are not embedded in the actual learning flow.

What this stage produces
  • A judgment of whether the curriculum produces intended learning for the full range of learners it serves
  • Identification of systemic weak points — not just component-level issues
  • A clear throughline from programme purpose to outcome to assessment to instruction to learner experience
  • A framework for ongoing review as learner performance data, faculty feedback, and contextual conditions evolve
Note: Setting measurable indicators aligned to equity goals is essential for tracking whether the curriculum is working. Indicators such as completion rates, assessment performance distributions, and learner-reported experience data all contribute. The curriculum map is a living system, not a completed document.
Section 4

Key decisions

No two curriculum mapping engagements look the same. The decisions an organisation makes about scope, depth, timing, and involvement determine what the work can accomplish and what it will produce. Understanding these dimensions supports more realistic planning and better use of design resources.

Six decision dimensions

🎯

Scope

What is actually being mapped?
  • Single course or module
  • Full programme or credential pathway
  • Institutional curriculum systems, policies, and practices
🔍

Depth

How thorough does the work need to be?
  • Rapid audit for initial prioritisation
  • Comprehensive review across all competency areas
  • Ongoing iterative review embedded in the design cycle
👥

Learner involvement

Are learners part of the design process?
  • Expert review only
  • Expert review with structured learner input
  • Co-design: learners actively shape outcomes, assessments, and redesign decisions
⏱️

Timing in the design cycle

When does mapping happen relative to development?
  • Pre-development: informing design before content is built
  • Pre-launch: catching issues before a programme goes live
  • Post-launch: addressing existing curricula through remediation and revision
📄

Reporting and use of findings

Who needs to act on the work, and how?
  • Technical documentation for designers and faculty
  • Strategic summary for leadership and governance
  • Capacity-building: sharing knowledge across the team through the mapping process itself
🏗️

Focus area

Where is the work primarily directed?
  • Outcomes and assessment equity
  • Instructional sequencing and learning design
  • Systems and governance: policy, procurement, and workflows
Rapid versus comprehensive approaches: Rapid reviews trade methodological depth for speed. They are useful for initial prioritisation when resources are constrained — provided findings are understood as a starting point, not a complete picture. Comprehensive mapping produces more trustworthy findings but requires genuine time investment from stakeholders. Neither approach is inherently superior; the question is what the context actually needs.

Governing principles

Several principles should govern the decisions above, regardless of scope, depth, or timing.

Equity as foundation
Equity is not a supplementary concern addressed after design decisions are made. It is the lens through which every outcome, assessment, strategy, and participation structure is examined from the outset. If a design choice maintains existing power imbalances without being essential to the learning itself, it should be questioned.
Construct first
At every decision point, the question is: what is this actually measuring, facilitating, or producing? Outcomes, assessments, and strategies that conflate the construct with the format — measuring a learner's ability to perform under constrained conditions rather than the intended capability — undermine the entire alignment chain.
Collaboration over authority
Better curriculum decisions emerge from genuine stakeholder collaboration. The most consequential input often comes from learners with direct experience of the curriculum's barriers. Subject matter expertise and lived experience are both necessary; neither alone is sufficient.
Evidence over assumption
Design decisions should be grounded in evidence about how people learn, what barriers exist, and whether the curriculum is producing intended outcomes. Intuition, convention, and administrative convenience are not adequate substitutes — and research consistently shows they produce curricula that work well for a narrow range of learners while disadvantaging others.
Living system, not document
A curriculum map is not a deliverable to be filed. It requires ongoing review as learner performance data accumulates, contexts change, and new evidence emerges. The maintenance cycle is not optional — it is what separates curriculum mapping as a professional practice from curriculum archaeology.
Section 5

Learner impact

Rigorous, equity-centred curriculum mapping creates change at two horizons. Shorter-term outcomes are tangible and inspectable. The longer-term shift affects how an organisation designs and delivers learning for everyone it serves — and what kind of learning culture it becomes.

Shorter term

What becomes visible and improvable

  • Gaps in the curriculum are identified and named — missing outcomes, under-developed sequences, and assessments that measure something other than the intended construct
  • Structural barriers are surfaced: outcomes that embed unnecessary language demands, assessments that disadvantage particular groups, and participation structures that reward a narrow range of expression
  • Learners who have been quietly underserved by the existing design become visible in the data, rather than attributed to individual deficit or lack of effort
  • Educators develop practical curriculum literacy through genuine engagement with the mapping process — not only those with formal curriculum design roles
  • The alignment between what is stated in the programme and what is actually experienced by learners becomes inspectable and improvable
Longer term

What becomes embedded in practice

  • Equity-centred design becomes a habit rather than a retrofit — reducing remediation costs and improving learning outcomes for the full range of learners the programme serves
  • Better structure, clearer language, flexible demonstration modes, and consistent expectations improve the experience for every learner — not only those for whom accommodations were originally designed
  • Organisations develop the institutional literacy to require and evaluate equity and accessibility in procurement decisions, new programme development, and platform selection
  • The focus shifts from individual accommodation toward proactive inclusion — affecting faculty development, governance practices, and organisational identity
  • Learner trust and persistence improve when assessments are transparently designed, criteria are clear, and formative support is built into the programme before high-stakes moments
On evidence and outcomes: Research consistently demonstrates that equity-centred curriculum design supports retention, participation, and completion. Inclusive design — grounded in learning science, structured around authentic performance, and built with genuine learner input — produces better educational environments for all learners. This is not a tradeoff between rigour and inclusion: they are the same goal, pursued through the same means.

What changes — and what requires sustained attention

Many inequities in curriculum are normalised. Designers often inherit structures they did not create, and those structures can feel like common sense because they have been repeated for so long. Genuine equity work often requires tradeoffs: it may challenge standardisation, expose institutional habits, or require more flexible and relational design than traditional curriculum models are set up to support.

This is why curriculum mapping must be understood as a systemic practice rather than a one-time deliverable. Barriers accumulate as programmes evolve, platforms change, and learner populations shift. Periodic review embedded in ongoing practice prevents the buildup of unaddressed issues and is more effective than any single comprehensive effort conducted in isolation.

The central question — for both individual design decisions and the curriculum as a whole — is: for whom does this work, and for whom does it create unnecessary difficulty? Answering that question honestly, and acting on the answer, is what distinguishes curriculum mapping as a rigorous professional practice from curriculum mapping as documentation.

Section 6

Reflection on current practice

This reflection is designed to surface useful questions about your organisation's current curriculum mapping practice. It is not scored — it is intended to support honest self-assessment and identify where focused attention may be most productive. Read each question and consider which response most honestly reflects your current situation.

Question 1 of 4

How does your organisation currently approach curriculum design and mapping?

  • We do not currently have a structured curriculum mapping process
  • We map curriculum at the course level when new programmes are launched
  • We conduct periodic programme-level mapping using a mix of approaches
  • Curriculum mapping is integrated into our ongoing design and review cycle
Question 2 of 4

To what extent does equity inform your curriculum design decisions?

  • Equity considerations are not yet formally part of our curriculum process
  • We are aware of equity as a concern but have not systematically integrated it into design
  • We review outcomes and assessments with equity in mind, though not yet consistently
  • Equity is a named design principle with specific practices embedded throughout our mapping process
Question 3 of 4

To what extent do learners inform your curriculum design and review?

  • Learners are not currently involved in curriculum design or review
  • We gather learner feedback informally, mainly when issues are raised
  • We use structured learner feedback as part of our review process
  • Learners are involved in shaping outcomes, assessments, and design decisions
Question 4 of 4

How would you describe the relationship between your curriculum map and practice?

  • Our curriculum documentation does not closely reflect what is actually taught and assessed
  • There is reasonable alignment in some areas but meaningful gaps in others
  • Our map reflects intended practice well; we are working to close gaps between intended and learned curriculum
  • The map is actively used to inspect, evaluate, and improve learner experience over time

Questions worth sitting with

Whichever responses you identified with, the following questions tend to be productive regardless of where your organisation currently sits in this work.

  • What specific outcomes does your current curriculum target — and how would you know if they were achieved for the full range of learners you serve?
  • Which learners are currently navigating barriers in the curriculum that the design has not named or addressed?
  • Where are the gaps between your intended curriculum, what is actually enacted, and what learners take away — and where are those gaps most consequential?
  • To what extent is structured reflection on learning built into your curriculum, and what would make it more reliable across instructors and cohorts?
  • Which elements of your curriculum are most dependent on individual knowledge or skill — and what would happen if key people were unavailable?
  • What would it take to treat the curriculum map as a living system rather than a periodic compliance deliverable?

Developed as a knowledge resource for learning designers, programme leads, and organisational leaders working toward rigorous, equitable curriculum mapping practice. This resource draws on scholarship in curriculum theory, the science of learning, adult learning theory, equity and social justice frameworks, Universal Design for Learning, assessment theory, and instructional systems design. It does not constitute learning design consultation or advice specific to any organisation's context.
